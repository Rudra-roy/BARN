Demo 01 · sensing

What the robot actually sees

No camera, no map — just one 2-D LiDAR firing hundreds of rays a spin, each returning the distance to the first thing it hits. That sparse ring of hit points is the entire picture the robot works from. Drag the robot around.

2-D LiDAR

A single laser scan

Each faint line is a laser ray; each dot is where it struck an obstacle or wall. Everything between the dots and the robot is inferred free space — everything beyond is unknown.

Demo 02 · mapping

From hit points to a clearance field

Those scattered hits get folded into an occupancy grid (which cells are blocked), and then into a distance field — for every cell, how far is the nearest obstacle, and which way is “away”. That field is what the planner and MPC lean on. Paint walls and watch it recompute.

distance transform

Clearance heatmap + gradient

Warm cells are close to a wall (dangerous); cool cells are open (safe). The little arrows point up-gradient — the direction that increases clearance fastest. The MPC uses exactly this arrow to push the robot off obstacles.

near wall mid open

Demo 03 · global planning

A* search — and the cost that shapes it

To find a route, A* explores cells in order of f = g + w·h: g = cost so far, h = estimated cost to the goal. Slide w from 0 and watch the search transform — from Dijkstra flooding everywhere, to A* aiming at the goal, to greedy beelining (fast, but it can miss the best route). Then turn on the clearance cost and watch the path pull to the center of corridors.

Demo 04 · local planning

The elastic band: a path pulled taut

A raw A* route staircases and hugs walls. The local planner treats it like a stretchy band — an internal tension straightens it, an obstacle repulsion pushes it toward open space — and lets it settle into a smooth, clearance-aware reference. Drag the obstacle through the band.

elastic band

Path relaxation

Faint line = the original jagged route. Solid line = the band after relaxation. Every point is nudged each tick by a blend of smoothing, repulsion, and a pull back toward the original.

drag the red obstacle · high tension = straighter, high repulsion = wider berth

Demo 05 · the controller

MPC: rolling the model forward

Here's how a prediction is generated. Starting from the robot's real state, the MPC applies a trial command, steps the motion model forward (xₙ₊₁ = xₙ + Δt·v·cosθ …), applies the next, and so on — N steps into the future. Push the prediction range up and watch the ghost chain reach further; push speed up and watch it slow itself in the corners.

receding horizon

Model rollout

Dashed purple = the reference. Each blue ghost is a predicted pose from rolling the model forward. The green mark is the single step actually sent to the wheels — the rest is recomputed next tick (the “receding horizon”).

Demo 06 · the safety net

The shield can only say “slower” or “stop”

An independent last check with no planner state. It sweeps the body forward over its stopping distance and, if anything blocks it, scales the whole command down to the largest collision-free version. Push the speed up, or drag the wall in.

Demo 07 · getting unstuck

When spinning fails, back out the way you came

Wedged in a pinch, a rectangular robot can't rotate — its corners hit the walls and the shield freezes it. The fix is backtracking: reverse along the breadcrumb trail it just drove (guaranteed clear) to open space, then re-plan. Run a full episode.
